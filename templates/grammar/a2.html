Review of A1 Grammar

Before diving into A2 grammar, make sure you have a solid understanding of the following A1 topics:

  • Alphabet and pronunciation
  • Basic sentence structure (SVO)
  • Nouns and articles (der, die, das; ein, eine, ein)
  • Present tense verb conjugation (regular and irregular verbs)
  • Personal pronouns (ich, du, er, sie, es, wir, ihr, sie/Sie)
  • Basic adjectives
  • Numbers
  • Basic questions (yes/no and W-questions)
  • Simple prepositions of place and time

If you need to review any of these topics, visit our A1 Grammar section.

Cases: Nominative, Accusative, and Dative

Cases indicate the function of a noun or pronoun in a sentence and affect the articles and adjective endings. In A2, we focus on three cases: Nominative, Accusative, and Dative.

Nominative Case

Question words: Wer? Was? (Who? What?)

Function: Subject of the sentence

Example:
Der Mann liest die Zeitung.
The man reads the newspaper.

Accusative Case

Question words: Wen? Was? (Whom? What?)

Function: Direct object

Example:
Ich sehe den Hund.
I see the dog.

Dative Case

Question word: Wem? (To whom?)

Function: Indirect object

Example:
Ich gebe dem Mann das Buch.
I give the man the book.

Changes in Definite Articles

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative der die das die
Accusative den die das die
Dative dem der dem den + -n*

* In the plural dative, most nouns add an "-n" at the end (if they don't already end in "-n" or "-s").

Changes in Indefinite Articles

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative ein eine ein keine
Accusative einen eine ein keine
Dative einem einer einem keinen
Examples

Nominative: Der Mann gibt der Frau das Buch. (The man gives the woman the book.)

Accusative: Der Mann gibt der Frau das Buch. (The man gives the woman the book.)

Dative: Der Mann gibt der Frau das Buch. (The man gives the woman the book.)

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs add meaning to the main verb, expressing possibility, necessity, desire, etc. They are conjugated and placed in the second position, while the main verb goes to the end of the sentence in its infinitive form.

The Six Modal Verbs

können

Meaning: can, to be able to

Example: Ich kann Deutsch sprechen.
I can speak German.

müssen

Meaning: must, to have to

Example: Du musst jetzt gehen.
You must go now.

dürfen

Meaning: may, to be allowed to

Example: Wir dürfen hier nicht parken.
We are not allowed to park here.

sollen

Meaning: should, ought to

Example: Sie soll ihre Hausaufgaben machen.
She should do her homework.

wollen

Meaning: want to

Example: Er will ein Buch lesen.
He wants to read a book.

mögen/möchten

Meaning: to like/would like to

Example: Sie möchten einen Kaffee trinken.
They would like to drink a coffee.

Perfect Tense (Perfekt)

The perfect tense is a common way to talk about the past in spoken German. It's formed with an auxiliary verb (haben or sein) and the past participle (Partizip II) of the main verb.

Formation with haben

When to use: Most verbs use haben

Formula: haben (conjugated) + past participle

Examples:

  • Ich habe ein Buch gelesen.
    I have read a book.
  • Du hast Fußball gespielt.
    You have played football.
  • Wir haben Pizza gegessen.
    We have eaten pizza.
Formation with sein

When to use: Verbs that indicate a change of location or state

Formula: sein (conjugated) + past participle

Examples:

  • Ich bin nach Berlin gefahren.
    I have gone to Berlin.
  • Du bist zu Hause geblieben.
    You have stayed at home.
  • Er ist aufgewacht.
    He has woken up.

Formation of Past Participle (Partizip II)

Regular Verbs

Formula: ge- + verb stem + -t

Examples:

  • machen → gemacht
  • spielen → gespielt
  • kaufen → gekauft
Irregular Verbs

Formula: ge- + changed verb stem + -en

Examples:

  • trinken → getrunken
  • lesen → gelesen
  • sehen → gesehen
  • gehen → gegangen
Verbs with -ieren

Formula: verb stem + -iert (no ge-)

Examples:

  • studieren → studiert
  • telefonieren → telefoniert
  • funktionieren → funktioniert
Remember: In the perfect tense, the auxiliary verb (haben or sein) comes in the second position, and the past participle goes to the end of the sentence.
